Tutorial 1

Explore the World of Molecules!

Chemistry Adventures with Molecular Models:
Step 1: Open PubChem. Gather Your Atoms: Choose your favourite-coloured balls (or candies!) to represent different atoms. Bonus points if you can eat them afterward!
Step 2: Connect the Dots: Use toothpicks (or spaghetti) to connect your atoms and form molecules. Can you make a water molecule? (Hint: You’ll need 2 hydrogen balls and 1 oxygen ball!)
Step 3: Snap a Pic: Share your creation on social media with #MoleculeMaster!
Congratulations, Young Chemist! You've successfully built your first molecular Masterpiece! Remember to keep experimenting with new molecules and share your creations with #MoleculeMaster. Your journey in chemistry is just beginning. Stay curious and get ready for more exciting adventures ahead! Happy exploring!

Tutorial 2

The Ultimate Friendship Guide!

Understanding Chemical Bonds:
Think of atoms as friends who love to bond! Just like friendships, their connections can vary in style and strength. Some atoms share their toys, while others may have more dramatic exchanges. Let’s explore the molecules different ways they can connect and form the incredible molecules that make up our universe!
  1. Covalent Bonds:These atoms share their toys! They’re like best buddies trading snacks.
  2. Ionic Bonds: When one atom steals a toy from another.It’s a dramatic friendship, but it works!
  3. Metallic Bonds: Picture a party where everyone shares everything. Chaos, but fun!
Fantastic work, young chemist! You've discovered how atoms form bonds like friendships: sharing toys in covalent bonds, having dramatic exchanges in ionic bonds, and enjoying a chaotic party in metallic bonds. Remember these fun connections as you continue your chemistry journey. Stay curious and keep exploring the wonders of science ahead!

Tutorial 3

ChemSketch!

Visualizing Molecular Orbitals Using ChemSketch:
Step 1:: Download ChemSketch Get Started: • Visit the chemdraw • Look for the free version of ChemSketch and download it. Follow the installation instructions specific to your operating system. Launch the Program: • Open ChemSketch after installation. You’re now ready to create some molecular magic!
Step 2: Build a Molecule Create a Simple Molecule: Building Benzene • In ChemSketch, select the "Draw" tool from the toolbar. • Click on the carbon atom symbol (C) to place six carbon atoms in a hexagonal arrangement. • Use the bond tool to connect the carbons with alternating single and double bonds (or use the aromatic bond tool for simplicity). • Add hydrogen atoms by clicking on the hydrogen atom symbol (H) and placing one hydrogen on each carbon. • Ensure your benzene ring looks like a hexagon with a circle inside, indicating resonance.
Step 3:Generate Molecular Orbitals Access the Orbital Visualization: • Once your molecule is built, go to the menu and select "Structure" > "Molecular Orbitals". • You might be prompted to install additional plugins or modules—follow the on-screen instructions to do this. Select DFT or Hartree-Fock: • Choose a method to visualize molecular orbitals. For simplicity, you can select a basic option like Hartree-Fock (HF) or Density Functional Theory (DFT). • Click on "Calculate" to start the orbital generation. Depending on the software's capabilities, this may take a moment.
Step 4: Analyse Orbital Shapes Visualize the Results: • After the calculation, ChemSketch will display the molecular orbitals. You’ll see various shapes representing different orbitals (e.g., σ, π, σ*, π*). • Use the rotation and zoom tools to get different perspectives of these orbitals. Observe the Orbital Shapes: • σ (sigma) Orbitals: Look for the bonding orbitals that are generally symmetrical around the bond axis. These orbitals are crucial for forming strong covalent bonds. • π (pi) Orbitals: Identify the orbitals that form above and below the plane of the molecule. In benzene, these orbitals contribute to the delocalization of electrons. • Antibonding Orbitals (σ and π)**: Note the shape of antibonding orbitals, which typically have a node (a region of zero electron density) between nuclei. These are less stable than their bonding counterparts. Relate to Bonding and Properties: • Discuss how the shape and energy of these orbitals affect molecular stability. For example, the delocalized π electrons in benzene contribute to its stability and unique reactivity. • Consider how understanding these orbitals helps in predicting properties like colour, reactivity, and phase behaviour in organic compounds.
Congratulations! You’ve just visualized and analysed molecular orbitals using ChemSketch. Understanding molecular orbitals enhances your grasp of chemical bonding and molecular properties. Keep experimenting with different molecules to see how their orbitals vary, and soon you’ll be a pro at interpreting the fascinating dance of electrons!
